  1. CLINICAL ACCURACY

     Can a lay person with no training execute this protocol with no backup?
     Is anything in the answer hidden behind EMS arrival or professional equipment?

     The safety_critical (SC) flag doubles the question's weight in final scoring.

[V2Q02]  Bleeding & Wounds  |  SC=false
QUESTION:
  How do you correctly apply direct pressure to control a bleeding wound?
REFERENCE (92 words):
  Place a clean cloth, sterile dressing, or folded pad firmly over the wound. Apply steady continuous pressure with the heel of your hand — do not lift it to check the wound, as this disrupts clot formation. If the dressing soaks through, add another on top without removing the first. Maintain pressure for at least 10 minutes without interruption. Raise the injured limb above heart level if possible and if no fracture is suspected. If bleeding is severe or fails to respond after 10 minutes on a limb, escalate to tourniquet application.


[V2Q03]  Bleeding & Wounds  |  SC=false
QUESTION:
  Why should you not lift a blood-soaked dressing from a wound to replace it with a fresh one?
REFERENCE (82 words):
  Removing a dressing that has soaked through disturbs the clot forming over the wound. Clot formation is the body's primary mechanism for stopping bleeding, so disrupting it causes the wound to rebleed and restarts the clotting process from zero. The correct action is to add a second dressing on top of the saturated one and continue applying firm pressure. Lifting the original dressing also risks introducing contamination. Only someone with direct ability to manage the wound surgically should remove dressings once applied.

[V2Q07]  Bleeding & Wounds  |  SC=false
QUESTION:
  Why is it important to keep a casualty with a significant bleed warm and as still as possible?
REFERENCE (86 words):
  Movement raises heart rate and blood pressure, which can dislodge a forming clot and restart bleeding. Cold causes peripheral blood vessels to constrict and diverts blood away from vital organs, accelerating the progression into shock. Keeping the person still reduces clot disruption and lowers oxygen demand on an already compromised circulation. Keeping them warm prevents hypothermia, which itself impairs the clotting cascade and worsens haemorrhage. Together, stillness and warmth slow the progression of hypovolaemic shock and preserve the window of time in which intervention can succeed.


[V2Q08]  Cardiac & Resuscitation  |  SC=TRUE  ** safety-critical, 2x weight **
QUESTION:
  What should you do if you find an adult who has collapsed, is unresponsive, and is not breathing normally?
REFERENCE (109 words):

[V2Q10]  Cardiac & Resuscitation  |  SC=false
QUESTION:
  What signs tell you that CPR chest compressions are being performed effectively?
REFERENCE (93 words):
  Effective compressions achieve a chest depression depth of at least 5 centimetres. The chest must fully recoil to its resting position after each compression — the rescuer's hands should remain in contact but not press down between compressions. Rate should be 100 to 120 per minute, roughly two compressions per second. Compressor fatigue significantly reduces depth within two minutes, so rescuers should swap every two minutes if a second person is available. Interruptions of any kind reduce coronary perfusion pressure built up by continuous compressions and must be minimised to under 10 seconds.


[V2Q11]  Cardiac & Resuscitation  |  SC=false
QUESTION:
  How should you use an AED when it arrives during a cardiac arrest resuscitation?
REFERENCE (104 words):
  Turn the AED on and follow its voice prompts without delay. Attach electrode pads to bare dry skin — one below the right collarbone, one on the left side of the chest below the armpit. Continue until the person shows signs of life or is unable to be revived.


[V2Q12]  Cardiac & Resuscitation  |  SC=false
QUESTION:
  Why is it recommended not to stop CPR to check for a pulse more frequently than every 2 minutes?
REFERENCE (89 words):
  Stopping compressions even briefly causes coronary and cerebral perfusion pressure to drop to zero immediately, undoing the build-up achieved by uninterrupted CPR. It takes several seconds of continuous compressions to re-establish meaningful perfusion pressure each time they restart. Frequent pauses therefore reduce the total effectiveness of resuscitation. Manual pulse checks are also unreliable — lay rescuers and trained clinicians both frequently misidentify pulse presence or absence under stress. The cost of the pause is certain; the diagnostic value of the check is not. Continuous compressions provide the best outcome.

[V2Q16]  Minor Injuries & General First Aid  |  SC=false
QUESTION:
  Why should you not rub an eye when there is a suspected foreign object in it?
REFERENCE (89 words):
  Rubbing drives a surface foreign body deeper into the conjunctiva or cornea, converting a superficial object into an embedded one. [V2Q23]  Trauma & Musculoskeletal  |  SC=false
QUESTION:
  What does the RICE method involve and when is it appropriate to use it?
REFERENCE (103 words):
  RICE stands for Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation. Rest means stopping activity and avoiding further use of the injured area. Ice means applying a cold pack wrapped in cloth for 20 minutes every 2 hours for the first 48 hours — never apply ice directly to skin. Compression means applying a firm elastic bandage to reduce swelling, checking that circulation in the fingers or toes is not impaired. Elevation means raising the injured limb above heart level to reduce fluid accumulation. RICE is appropriate for soft tissue injuries such as sprains and strains. It is not appropriate for suspected fractures, dislocations, or open wounds.

[V2Q26]  Neurological & Altered Consciousness  |  SC=false
QUESTION:
  Why should a person who has sustained a concussion not return to sport or vigorous activity on the same day?
REFERENCE (95 words):
  After a concussion the brain is in a metabolically vulnerable state with reduced blood flow and impaired energy regulation. QUESTION:
  How do you recognise the difference between hypoglycaemia and hyperglycaemia in a conscious person with known diabetes?
REFERENCE (103 words):
  Hypoglycaemia develops rapidly over minutes with pale sweaty skin, shakiness, confusion, irritability, hunger, and palpitations. Blood glucose is below 4 mmol/L. [V2Q35]  Bites, Stings & Envenomation  |  SC=false
QUESTION:
  Why should you avoid cutting, sucking, applying a tourniquet to, or washing a snake bite wound?
REFERENCE (101 words):
  Cutting and sucking remove negligible amounts of venom while causing direct tissue damage and infection risk. Tourniquets cut off blood flow to the limb causing ischaemic damage, while doing nothing to prevent venom spread — Australian snake venom travels via the lymphatic system, not the bloodstream. Pressure immobilisation works by slowing lymphatic flow and is the only evidence-based physical first aid for Australian snake bite. None of the contraindicated measures improve outcomes.

[V2Q38]  Burns & Environmental Emergencies  |  SC=false
QUESTION:
  Why should you not apply butter, toothpaste, oil, or ice directly to a burn?
REFERENCE (105 words):
  Butter, oils, and toothpaste trap heat in burned tissue and prevent the cooling that running water provides. They act as culture media for bacteria, increasing infection risk, and contaminate the wound surface, complicating clinical assessment and treatment. They must be physically removed before medical treatment can proceed, which is painful and causes further tissue disruption. Ice and ice water cause vasoconstriction in burn margins, reducing blood flow to tissue that might otherwise survive, and risk causing frostbite on top of the burn — particularly dangerous in children and elderly people. Cool running water for 20 minutes is the only evidence-based immediate cooling treatment for burns.

  What are the signs of carbon monoxide poisoning and what should you do if you suspect it?
REFERENCE (111 words):
  Carbon monoxide poisoning presents as headache, dizziness, nausea, confusion, and fatigue — easily mistaken for flu but without fever. Multiple people or pets affected simultaneously in the same space strongly suggests CO. Do not enter the contaminated environment without breathing apparatus. Move the person to fresh air immediately if safe. Ventilate the space by opening doors and windows from outside if possible. Once in fresh air, lay the person down and monitor breathing closely — CO binds tightly to haemoglobin and delayed or worsening effects occur even after apparent recovery outdoors. If unresponsive, begin CPR immediately. CO poisoning requires oxygen therapy and medical assessment; seek evacuation to a medical facility urgently.
