QUESTION:  Why should you not lift a blood-soaked dressing from a wound to replace it with a fresh one?
REFERENCE: Removing a dressing that has soaked through disturbs the clot forming over the wound. Clot formation is the body's primary mechanism for stopping bleeding, so disrupting it causes the wound to rebleed and restarts the clotting process from zero. The correct action is to add a second dressing on top of the saturated one and continue applying firm pressure. Lifting the original dressing also risks introducing contamination. Only someone with direct ability to manage the wound surgically should remove dressings once applied.

QUESTION:  Why is it important to keep a casualty with a significant bleed warm and as still as possible?
REFERENCE: Movement raises heart rate and blood pressure, which can dislodge a forming clot and restart bleeding. Keeping them warm prevents hypothermia, which itself impairs the clotting cascade and worsens haemorrhage. Together, stillness and warmth slow the progression of hypovolaemic shock and preserve the window of time in which intervention can succeed.

QUESTION:  Why should you not apply butter, toothpaste, oil, or ice directly to a burn?
REFERENCE: Butter, oils, and toothpaste trap heat in burned tissue and prevent the cooling that running water provides. They act as culture media for bacteria, increasing infection risk, and contaminate the wound surface, complicating clinical assessment and treatment. They must be physically removed before medical treatment can proceed, which is painful and causes further tissue disruption. Ice and ice water cause vasoconstriction in burn margins, reducing blood flow to tissue that might otherwise survive, and risk causing frostbite on top of the burn — particularly dangerous in children and elderly people. Cool running water for 20 minutes is the only evidence-based immediate cooling treatment for burns.
