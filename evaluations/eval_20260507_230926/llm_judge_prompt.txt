QUESTION: A motorcyclist has crashed and you suspect a spinal injury. They are breathing and wearing a helmet. What should you do about the helmet?
REFERENCE: Do not remove the helmet if the person is breathing and the helmet is not blocking the airway. Call 000. Keep the person still and manually stabilise the head and neck in the position found. Open the visor or loosen the chin strap only if needed to help breathing. Remove the helmet only if the airway cannot be managed otherwise, ideally with trained help while maintaining spinal alignment.

[10CAT_4BIT]: If the helmet is not causing a neck or head injury, leave it on. It may be providing some protection. However, if it's causing pain or movement, remove it to avoid further injury.

[10CAT_4BIT_2]: If the helmet is not causing a neck or head injury, leave it on. It may help stabilize the neck and reduce further damage. Keep the casualty in a comfortable position and monitor breathing and pulse.

[10CAT_4BIT_3]: If the helmet is not on, place it on the casualty’s head and secure it with a scarf or cloth. Check for any visible damage to the helmet and ensure it is not blocking the airway. If the helmet is damaged or not properly secured, remove it and place the casualty in the lateral position. Ensure the airway remains open and monitor their breathing closely.

[10CAT_4BIT_4]: If the casualty is breathing and wearing a helmet, do not remove it unless there's an immediate danger (e.g., fire). Support the head and neck, and keep the casualty still while waiting for emergency services.

[10CAT_4BIT_5]: If the helmet is not secured or the person is unconscious, remove it to allow for proper breathing. If the helmet is too tight, loosen it slightly to allow for better circulation. If the person is in severe pain, keep them calm and still, and seek medical help immediately.

[10CAT_4BIT_6]: If the helmet is not causing pressure on the head and neck, leave it on. If it is causing pressure, remove it for comfort and to avoid further injury. Keep the person calm and monitor their breathing and pulse.

[10CAT_4BIT_7]: If the helmet is not causing a neck or head injury, leave it on. It will help stabilize the head and reduce further damage. Keep the person calm and monitor their breathing and pulse.

Q29 [SC] | Category: Spinal Injuries & Patient Movement
QUESTION: A person with a suspected spinal injury starts vomiting while lying on their back. How should rescuers move them?
REFERENCE: Call emergency services. Protect the airway while maintaining spinal alignment. If they must be turned, use a log-roll with several rescuers: one person stabilises the head and neck while others roll the shoulders, hips, and legs together as one unit. Do not twist the neck or back. Clear vomit from the mouth and continue monitoring breathing.

[10CAT_4BIT]: If the casualty is vomiting and lying on their back, rescuers should move them carefully to avoid causing further injury to the spine. They should support the head and neck, and move the casualty slowly while minimizing movement of the spine.

[10CAT_4BIT_2]: If the casualty is vomiting and lying on their back, rescuers should move them to a lateral position while maintaining the head, neck, and spine in alignment. This prevents the vomit from entering the airway and ensures that the casualty remains stable while waiting for medical assistance.

[10CAT_4BIT_3]: If the person is vomiting, place them in the lateral position (on their side) to prevent choking. Ensure the airway remains open and monitor their breathing closely. Do not move them unless necessary for safety. Seek immediate medical help.

[10CAT_4BIT_4]: If the casualty is unconscious and vomiting, rescuers should carefully turn them onto their side while supporting the head and neck. This prevents choking and ensures the airway remains clear.

[10CAT_4BIT_5]: If the person is vomiting, place them in the lateral position (on their side) to prevent choking. Ensure their airway remains open and monitor their breathing closely. Avoid moving them unless necessary for safety.

[10CAT_4BIT_6]: If the casualty is vomiting and lying on their back, rescuers should move them to a side position to prevent choking. This ensures that the vomit is not ingested into the lungs, which can cause further injury.

[10CAT_4BIT_7]: If the casualty is vomiting and lying on their back, rescuers should move them to a semi-reclined position, keeping the head and neck aligned with the spine. This position helps prevent further injury to the spine and allows for easier monitoring of breathing and circulation.

Q30 | Category: Minor Injuries & General First Aid
QUESTION: What is the correct first aid for a nosebleed?
REFERENCE: Sit the person upright and lean them forward. Pinch the soft part of the nose just below the bridge continuously for 10-15 minutes. Encourage breathing through the mouth and spitting out blood rather than swallowing it. Do not tilt the head back. Apply a cold pack to the bridge of the nose if helpful. Seek medical care if bleeding lasts more than 20 minutes, follows a head injury, or is very heavy.

[10CAT_4BIT]: To treat a nosebleed, instruct the person to lean slightly forward and pinch their nostrils together. This helps to stop the bleeding. If the bleeding continues or if the person feels dizzy, seek medical help.

[10CAT_4BIT_2]: To treat a nosebleed, instruct the person to lean slightly forward and pinch their nostrils together. This helps to stop blood flow. If the bleeding continues, repeat the process. If the bleeding persists or if the person feels dizzy, seek medical help.

[10CAT_4BIT_3]: To stop a nosebleed, pinch the nostrils together for about 10 seconds. This will help to seal the blood vessels in the nose. If the bleeding does not stop, apply a cold compress to the bridge of the nose and seek medical help.

[10CAT_4BIT_4]: To stop a nosebleed, lean the person forward and pinch their nostrils together. Apply gentle pressure for 10 minutes. If the bleeding continues, repeat the process. If the bleeding stops, tilt the person's head back and lean to the side to allow fluid to drain.

[10CAT_4BIT_5]: To stop a nosebleed, pinch the nostrils together for about 10 seconds. If the bleeding does not stop, tilt the head back and pinch the nostrils together for 10 seconds again. If the bleeding continues, apply a cold compress to the bridge of the nose and seek medical help.

[10CAT_4BIT_6]: To treat a nosebleed, have the person sit or lean forward slightly. Pinch the bridge of their nose and gently pull it upwards to stop the bleeding. If the bleeding continues, apply a cold compress to the area to reduce swelling. If the bleeding persists or if there is excessive blood loss, seek medical help.

[10CAT_4BIT_7]: To perform a nosebleed, have the person sit or lean forward slightly. Pinch the nostrils together and gently hold for 10-15 minutes. If bleeding continues, repeat the process. If bleeding persists or if there is excessive blood loss, seek medical help.
